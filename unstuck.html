The mechanics — why you avoid, and how to interrupt it

🔁 The avoidance loop

Every time you dodge a scary task, you get an instant hit of relief. That relief is a reward, and it trains your brain — this is negative reinforcement, the same mechanism that maintains phobias. The task didn't get less scary; avoiding it just got more automatic.

Meanwhile the avoided thing grows in the dark. You never collect evidence that it's manageable, so your imagination fills the gap with worst cases.

The interrupt: brief contact. Thirty seconds of opening the envelope gives your brain disconfirming evidence — "oh, that was survivable" — and the loop starts weakening. You don't have to finish anything. You just have to touch it.

🧠 The ADHD "now vs not-now" brain

ADHD isn't a knowledge problem — you know exactly what you should do. It's a performance problem at the point of action. Executive function differences mean future consequences (a late fee in 2 weeks, a health issue in 10 years) barely register against anything happening now. Russell Barkley calls it "time blindness": there's now and not-now, and not-now doesn't exist.

The interrupt: stop relying on internal motivation and externalise everything. Make tasks physical and visible (the bill on the keyboard, gym bag by the door), make deadlines artificial and near ("before dinner tonight"), and shrink every task until the first step fits inside "now" — under five minutes.

😰 It's about the feeling, not the task

Procrastination research (Sirois & Pychyl) is clear: we don't avoid tasks, we avoid emotions — the anxiety in the bill, the vulnerability in the checkup, the boredom in the paperwork. "Give in to feel good": you trade tomorrow's problem for tonight's relief.

That's why time-management systems keep failing you — they solve the wrong problem. The bill isn't a scheduling issue; it's a dread issue.

The interrupt: name the emotion out loud ("I'm avoiding this because I'm scared of the number"). Affect labelling measurably reduces the emotion's grip. Then ask: can I tolerate this feeling for five minutes? You almost always can.

🌙 Revenge bedtime procrastination

Staying up too late scrolling isn't a discipline failure — researchers call it revenge bedtime procrastination: when the day gives you no free time, you steal it from sleep. The busier and more depleted the day (sound familiar?), the stronger the pull. And it's self-defeating, because the sleep debt worsens tomorrow's executive function, which worsens tomorrow's avoidance.

The interrupt: don't target the scrolling, target the trigger — schedule actual leisure earlier in the evening so there's nothing to "steal back". Then make bedtime an if-then routine (when the episode ends, phone goes on the charger in the kitchen), not a nightly negotiation.

🩺 Why we dodge checkups and exercise

Health avoidance has an extra twist: the task threatens your identity, not just your time. A checkup risks bad news; a run risks feeling unfit. Avoiding them protects the self-image today at compound interest — you of all people know how that story ends clinically.

The interrupt: reduce every health task to a booking, not an event. You're not "dealing with your health" today — you're sending one email or making one two-minute call to book a slot weeks away. Future-you handles the appointment; today-you only has to dial. For exercise, the commitment is putting shoes on and getting out the door — ten minutes counts, and usually continues by itself.

⚡ Stop it in its tracks: the 4-step interrupt

When you notice yourself deflecting off a task (suddenly checking email, tidying, "researching"):

1. Name it. "I'm avoiding X because it makes me feel Y." Out loud if you can.

2. Shrink it. What version of this fits in five minutes? Not "sort the bills" — "open one envelope".

3. Start the timer. Permission to stop at five minutes, fully guilt-free. (The Today tab does this.)

4. Be kind afterwards. Whatever happened, no self-flagellation — self-forgiveness is what prevents the next avoidance, and that's an empirical finding, not a nicety.
